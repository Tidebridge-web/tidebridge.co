Gold’s Oldest Habit

In every major crisis of the past half-century, gold has fallen first and recovered later. The correction of 2026 — a quarter off January’s record — is following a script older than most careers on a trading floor.

In this issue

I.  Fifty years, eight storms, one pattern — gold falls first in every crisis. What happened next, from 1973 to 2022.

II.  The engine room — real interest rates, not headlines, have driven every gold cycle since 1970. Where that gauge sits today.

III.  Anatomy of a liquidation — who is actually selling: leverage, machines, ETFs, and a new kind of distress seller.

IV.  The buyer who doesn’t read the tape — why central banks became, after 2022, the bid that no previous cycle had.

V.  What the long view teaches — three lessons from the record, stated plainly.

Gold was supposed to be the asset that protects you when the world catches fire. In February 2026 the world duly caught fire — missiles over the Gulf, oil up sharply, tankers idling outside the Strait of Hormuz — and gold fell. From a record near $5,600 an ounce in late January, the metal has retreated to the low $4,000s, its weakest level since last November. For anyone who bought the headline version of gold, the experience has been bewildering. For anyone who has read its history, it is almost reassuringly familiar.

This letter takes the long view: what gold has actually done across fifty years of geopolitical and monetary stress, why it behaves the way it does, and what is genuinely different about the present cycle. No price targets, no portfolio advice — just the historical record, which turns out to be remarkably consistent.

I. Fifty years, eight storms, one pattern

Begin with the chart every gold investor should commit to memory.

Plate I. Gold, 1970–2026, annual averages, logarithmic scale. Indicative data: LBMA, author calculations.

Now look closer at the crises themselves: the OPEC embargo of 1973, the Iranian Revolution of 1979, the Gulf War of 1991, the September 11 attacks, and the washouts of 2016, 2018, 2020 and 2022. In each case the popular assumption — that gold rises when the world darkens — failed on contact. Gold fell on the outbreak of the Gulf War. It gave back its 9/11 spike within weeks. It surrendered the entire Ukraine-invasion premium of March 2022 inside six months. The reason is mechanical rather than mysterious: in the opening phase of any crisis, the world wants dollars. Margin calls arrive, portfolios shrink, and the most liquid, most profitable position in the book becomes the cash machine. Gold is sold precisely because it has done its job.

Plate II. Gold’s drawdown in each crisis episode, and its performance in the twelve months that followed the low. Approximate spot/London fix data; author calculations. Past performance is not indicative of future results.

What happened next is the other half of the habit. From the 1973 and 1979 lows, gold roughly doubled within a year. The shallower episodes produced gentler but still substantial recoveries. The honest exceptions are on the chart too: 1991, when gold went nowhere for years, and 2016, when the first year’s recovery was modest after a four-year bear market. The pattern is not a law of nature. But across eight episodes, the crisis dip has far more often marked an opportunity than a warning — and the deepest, most violent dips have preceded the strongest recoveries.

II. The engine room: real interest rates

If war doesn’t drive gold, what does? Strip away the headlines and one variable explains most of the past fifty years: the real interest rate — what cash actually earns after inflation. Gold pays no coupon. When cash earns more than inflation, holding gold carries a real cost, and the metal struggles. When cash earns less than inflation, gold’s “defect” disappears — everything else is losing purchasing power too, and the asset with no yield but no counterparty becomes the asset with no apology.

Plate III. Gold (blue line, log scale, right) against the real short rate — the Fed funds rate minus CPI inflation (dark line, left; blue shading marks negative-rate years). Indicative annual data: Federal Reserve, BLS, LBMA, author calculations.

The chart reads like a biography. The 1970s: oil shocks pushed inflation up faster than the central bank’s nominal response, real rates went deeply negative, and gold rose seventeen-fold in a decade — not because of the wars themselves, but because policy persistently lagged the inflation the wars created. Then 1980: Paul Volcker forced real rates violently positive, and gold entered a twenty-year hibernation through every crisis the 1980s and 1990s could supply — including the Gulf War, which is why 1991 is the exception in Plate II. The 2000s and 2010s: real rates pinned near or below zero, and gold quietly compounded. And the right-hand edge of the chart shows the present configuration: a policy rate in the mid-threes against consumer inflation running above four — the real short rate slipping back below zero, in the same month gold corrected by a quarter.

History’s lesson here is precise. It is not that conflict lifts gold; 1991 disproves that. It is that supply-shock inflation which outruns the policy response lifts gold — and that the lifting tends to begin from exactly the kind of liquidation low that conflict initially produces. The 1970s remain the canonical case: gold’s greatest decade began, twice, with a double-digit drawdown.

III. Anatomy of a liquidation: who is actually selling

A correction of this size invites a question the headlines rarely answer: who, exactly, is selling? The composition matters, because history treats different sellers very differently.

The first seller is the leveraged investor. Gold entered this year as one of the most widely held and most profitable positions in global portfolios; when volatility surged across rates, currencies and equities this spring, risk models everywhere demanded smaller books, and the position that gets sold in that moment is the one with a profit and a deep, liquid market. Gold became the cash machine — the same role it played in the liquidations of 2008 and 2020. The second seller is the machine itself: trend-following strategies that had accumulated near-record exposure on the way up now reverse mechanically as their signals roll over, selling without reference to value. The third is the ETF investor — global gold funds surrendered roughly eighty tonnes in a matter of weeks this spring, the retail echo of the institutional unwind. The fourth is the most novel: a handful of oil-importing central banks, their currencies strained by the energy shock, have been selling reserves to buy dollars and fuel — distress supply that is, by construction, tied to the oil price itself. And beneath all of it sits the one genuinely fundamental seller: the investor responding to the prospect of cash yields competing with inflation again.

The record’s verdict on this composition is worth stating. Declines dominated by forced and mechanical selling have historically been the ones that reversed fastest — once the leverage is cleared and the machines have turned, no seller remains beneath the market. The recoveries of 1974 and 2020 followed liquidations of exactly this shape. What distinguishes a durable bear market from a violent cleaning is never the depth of the fall; it is whether the fundamental seller — the real-rate ledger of Section II — stays in charge once the noise has passed.

IV. The buyer who doesn’t read the tape

One feature of the present cycle has no precedent in the fifty-year record, and it argues for paying attention rather than turning away. In every previous gold bull market, the official sector — central banks — was a seller or an absentee. Through the 1990s, European central banks disposed of thousands of tonnes near the lows. The buyer of last resort did not exist.

Plate IV. Net central bank gold purchases, tonnes. The dashed line marks 2022, when roughly $300 billion of one country’s reserve assets were frozen — and annual official buying roughly doubled. Indicative data: World Gold Council, author calculations.

That changed in 2022, when the freezing of Russia’s reserve assets demonstrated that a sovereign’s savings, held in someone else’s currency, are conditional. Official purchases roughly doubled to about a thousand tonnes a year and have stayed near that pace since; by late 2025, the gold held by non-US central banks was worth more than their entire holdings of US Treasuries. This buyer is strategic, price-insensitive and slow. It does not read the tape. In the current correction, a handful of oil-importing countries have sold reserves to defend their currencies — distress supply that is, by construction, temporary — while the strategic accumulators have continued to add. A structural bid of this kind existed in none of the eight prior episodes. It does not abolish volatility, as this spring has demonstrated. It changes what stands beneath it.

V. What the long view teaches

The record allows humility and patience at the same time, and it can be stated plainly. First: gold falling in the opening phase of a crisis is the historical norm, not evidence that the asset is broken — the dip is the pattern. Second: the variable worth watching is not the news flow but the gap between inflation and the policy rate; every durable gold advance of the past fifty years has been built on that gap, and every durable decline on its closure. Third: corrections of 15–25% have occurred inside every major gold bull market on record — including five separate double-digit drawdowns on the road from $250 to $1,900 between 2001 and 2011. Volatility has always been the price of admission, and the investors history rewarded were those who understood which regime they were in, rather than those who reacted to its loudest days.

The 2026 correction will resolve as they all do — in time, with the real-rate ledger as the referee. The purpose of the long view is simply to make sure that, when it does, the pattern comes as recognition rather than surprise.
